Renters' Rights Act 2026: What Every Landlord Must Do Before 1 May

Published 8 April 2026 by ComplianceCrib

The Renters' Rights Act 2025 takes effect on 1 May 2026. It is the biggest change to private tenancy law in England since the Housing Act 1988. If you are a private landlord, there are things you must do before and after that date, and the penalties for getting them wrong are serious.

This is not a summary of the entire Act. This is a practical guide to the specific actions you need to take, the deadlines you need to meet, and the fines you face if you miss them.

The Information Sheet: your first deadline

If you have an existing tenant on a written tenancy agreement (an AST) that started before 1 May 2026, you must provide them with the government's official Information Sheet by 31 May 2026. This is a document produced by MHCLG that explains how the new rules affect their tenancy.

You can download it from GOV.UK and send it to your tenant by email or post. You must send it to every named tenant on the agreement.

If you don't provide the Information Sheet by 31 May 2026, you face a civil penalty of up to £7,000. For repeated or continuing breaches, the fine can reach £40,000.

If your tenancy is based on a verbal agreement (no written contract), the Information Sheet does not apply to you. Instead, you must provide a written statement of the key tenancy terms by 31 May 2026. The penalty for failing to do so is the same.

Section 21 is gone

From 1 May 2026, you cannot serve a Section 21 "no-fault" eviction notice. Any Section 21 notice served on or after that date is invalid. If you need to regain possession of your property, you must use the reformed Section 8 grounds.

If you have already served a valid Section 21 notice before 1 May 2026, it remains valid for its normal lifespan, but you must apply to court for a claim form by 31 July 2026.

Rent increases change completely

From 1 May 2026, the only way to increase rent is through the Section 13 process using the new Form 4A (available on GOV.UK from 1 May). The key changes:

You can only increase rent once per year. You must give at least two months' notice (up from one month). Existing rent review clauses in tenancy agreements are no longer enforceable. Tenants can challenge any increase at the First-tier Tribunal.

If you try to increase rent using the old process, a rent review clause, or an informal agreement, the increase is unenforceable.

Tenants can request pets

From 1 May, tenants have the right to request permission to keep a pet. You must respond within four weeks. You cannot unreasonably refuse. Blanket "no pets" clauses in tenancy agreements are void.

No more rental bidding

You must publish a single asking rent on any written property advertisement. You cannot ask for, encourage, or accept bids above the advertised price. Advance rent is capped at one month, payable only after the tenancy agreement is signed.

What's coming later in 2026

Two further requirements are expected later in the year, though exact dates have not been confirmed:

The PRS Database will require all private landlords to register themselves and their properties. If you don't register, you lose access to most possession grounds. The PRS Landlord Ombudsman will require mandatory membership, giving tenants a free route to complain about landlord conduct.

What to do right now

If you have tenants on written ASTs: download and send the Information Sheet from GOV.UK before 31 May. If you have tenants on verbal agreements: provide a written statement of terms before 31 May. Do not serve any Section 21 notices from 1 May. Prepare for the new rent increase process using Form 4A. Remove blanket pet ban clauses from your tenancy templates.
